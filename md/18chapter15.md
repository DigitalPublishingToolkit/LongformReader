# Girl Math Fundamentals – Proof by Subversion

## Irma Mastenbroek

### Originally published November 20th, 2025

“All models are wrong, but some are useful.”[^18chapter15_1] George Box - *“Models
are stupid and we need more of them.” Paul Smaldino *

Girl Math allowed girlies who just spent a little too much money to
rationalise their purchases by framing them as financially sensible,
even when they weren't, back on the internet in 2023. According to this
logic, a girl earns income when a friend cancels dinner plans or when
she returns a dress to the store. The reverse is also true. If something
is heavily discounted and she decides not to buy it, she'd be sadly
missing out on 'free' money. The girl concludes her argument by teasing
"Girl Math!" and then goes about her day, enjoying her purchase
guilt-free.

Contrary to what the name might suggest, Girl Math is not gender or
age-bound. It’s an inclusive tool, an attitude to life, that anyone can
use to make life more enjoyable. Acknowledging the humour and irony
shaping the argument, one can easily find this indulgent,
consumption-oriented attitude relatable. Girl Math is familiar,
obnoxious, and justifies everything, and therefore, we love it. Beyond
this market’s logical illogical nonsense, Girl Math offers a deeper,
more complex unfolding of a mathematical paradigm that has been
historically oppressed. Indeed, with these emergent practices of logic,
consumers and girlies get an opportunity to reflect on how 'making
sense' and 'rational thinking' can inhabit a deeper sense of intuition
and reasoning. There is something counterintuitive, almost blasphemous,
about Girl Math, as it juxtaposes the hegemony of a system of reasoning
that, for a long time, has controlled the narrative around what is
rationally acceptable.

As both a mathematician and a girl, I’ll admit I didn't know whether to
laugh or cry when I first encountered the Girl Math phenomenon. It
reminded me of a pink kid's T-shirt I once saw in a store that said:
"Too Pretty to Do Math." I wasn't sure which part of me was more

reassembled a threat to feminism by supporting the patriarchal
misconception that girls have to be pretty, agreeable, stupid, and, most
of all, bad at math. On surface level, it might look like Girl Math is
doing the same. It goes without saying that the cliche that a girl is
too cute to do math is wrong. This is a patriarchal rhetoric that is so

## Girls Always Knew Something About Pure Math

What do I know about this? Well, I am a math girly myself. I didn't get
into this field because I was so disciplined, organised, or
hard-working. In fact, I was quite the opposite. I was a chaotic child.
I would forget about exams, lose my books, and never do my homework. In
most classes, you had to accept the rules of the game: memorising
history dates, conjugating French verbs you will never use, and nod
politely at the idea that GDP is a serious measure of well-being. Quite
the nightmare. I couldn't help but be critical of all these assumptions.
Who decided this? Why that? What if I disagree? Pure math, however,
barely asked me to memorise or accept anything blindly. It did not force
itself upon reality, nor did it punish me for my lack of obedience. Pure
math rewarded stubbornness.

Interestingly enough, there's a common assumption that maths is one of
the most dictatorial disciplines, as it presupposes and imposes strict,
regulated rules and manners. However, to my understanding and practice
of mathematics, pure math is a much more speculative, playful, and
poetic field of reasoning. In pure math, problems are solved not because
we need or want to know the answer for any practical reason. They are
solved just for the fuck of it, because we enjoy the puzzle. Like
solving a difficult Sudoku. Essentially, we do it just for funzies.

The commonly encountered applied math doctrine that is oppressive and
non-negotiable will, from here on, be referred to as Boy Math. It is a
logic that is rarely structurally argued but instead imposed. Formulas
are supposed to come flying from the air and be accepted. Refuse to
comply, and you'll be overruled. The girl makes fun of this kingdom by
opposing their super-duper profound big-boi logic. This is no joke to
boys. They are so serious about logic. They can't help but respond very
emotionally to Girl Math statements. Of course, the girl fully
anticipated their frustrations, but she wasn't triggered. In fact, she
was hoping for this response. After all, boys not getting it is part of
the joke. This is where it started to dawn on me that the girl is not
only funny but also making a valid mathematical point. *The girls who
get it, get it.*

What are girls trying to do here? Pissing boys off by bothering them
with illogical nonsense? Yes. But the girls are making another
observation: keeping emotions and general vibes out of the math equation
is a design choice that could well be undone. With Girl Math, the girls
are telling the world: *we don’t really care about your numbers and
formulas. For us, the vibes matter. Numbers are fake, intuition is
real.*

In that exercise, we interlock our fingers and enter this thought castle

### Pure Math?

How does this translate to Girl Math, then? Where to start? In pure
mathematics, we begin by choosing axioms, definitions, and rules of
inference. We decide what we define as a point, a line, or a set. We
determine what it means to add, to fold, to rotate. Then we explore the
consequences of those choices. The entire edifice is built on our
starting assumptions (axioms), which are arbitrary. If the mathematical
text suggests 'let's assume xyz', and you, for some reason, snap back
with a 'well, how about let's not?', that's totally okay. You only have
to continue reading if you're interested in what happens, or if you're
happy to assume what we just suggested. If not, all good. Axioms are
pure assumptions, meaning that they explicitly do not have to be true;
they might well be false… It doesn't really matter.

So, there's no need to prove assumptions or even justify why they would
make any sense. If you wish to assume some nonsense, such as ‘nothing
that only men like is cool’ or '0=1', math won't stop you. Math will
say, 'You do you, queen. Let's run with that’. Quick enough, you would
discover that assuming 0=1 makes all numbers disappear, and that the
entire number line would collapse into one point. But worry not, you
have not broken math, but instead built the smallest, flattest possible
mathematical world where distinctions vanish, but some structure
remains. Even this degenerate universe counts as mathematics. So then,
how have mathematicians come up with their assumptions and definitions
if they can assume and define anything they'd like? Well, that's the
unfolding of history: mathematicians assumed things that allowed them to
create a world in which the things they wished to be true could be true.

Chances are, my dear reader, it took you by surprise to learn that math
is not objective, nor inherently 'true'. You might even have felt a bit
of resistance to the idea: a knot in your stomach or a nagging feeling
of disbelief. After all, we've been taught to trust numbers, formulas,
and proofs as universal truths. However, math is not static but
constantly mutating, and it doesn't always look "logical" when
perceived. Take the square root of –1. For ages, everyone agreed that it
was impossible by definition: a square is always positive. Negative
times negative is positive; positive times positive is positive. So, of
course, no real number squared could ever give you –1. Trying to take
its square root just seemed stupid. Then, one day, some random dude,
namely Euler, contested and posed, *"Okay, but what if I just call it
'i' and say i×i=−1?”*. At the time, other mathematicians basically
rolled their eyes: *“Dude, that’s not a number.”* He doubled
down: *“Okay, fine, it’s not a real number, shall we call it an
imaginary number?”* To which the math establishment replied: *“For the
love of God, please shut up.” *And yet, once Euler’s “*i”* was admitted
into the math club, previously impossible calculations started
unravelling beautifully. Entire families of polynomial equations that
had no solutions before suddenly opened up. Centuries later, these
"imaginary" numbers revealed themselves to be so useful that, without
them, we would not have the mathematics needed for radios, computers,
and cell phones. They have since been renamed complex numbers, because
how can we justify calling them "imaginary" (explicitly not genuine)
when they are used in real-world problems?

### Holy numbers

So, what about those real numbers —how real are they? Real numbers
consist of all numbers on the number line. That seems, perhaps, quite
unambiguous. However, there was a time when only rational numbers were
allowed. We must go back to the Pythagoreans, a cult-like group of
ancient Greek mathematicians who believed that whole numbers were holy,
divine, and connected to the gods. They also fiercely believed they had
proven that all numbers were rational. Why? Because they thought they
had shown that every point on the number line could be expressed as a
ratio of whole numbers, leaving no gaps. Their reasoning was elegant:
suppose a gap existed between two rational points, a/b and c/d. Then the
midpoint (a/b + c/d)/2 would also be a rational number in the gap. That
means our gap is now cut in half, and we can continue this process
forever until all gaps are filled, contradicting our assumption that a
gap existed. That was until Hippasus of Metapontum (a Pythagorean
himself) discovered that the hypotenuse of a 1 by 1 square, c =
sqrt(2)​, cannot be written as a ratio of integers. In other words,
sqrt(2)​ was suddenly *irrational*. This shattered the Pythagorean
worldview: if not all numbers were rational, then the divine order they
worshipped had cracks. Hippasus of Metapontum's discovery was
controversial. According to Vinay Kathotia in the article "The
Unreasonable Man," the gods, or the Pythagoreans, drowned him at sea, or
hundreds of oxen were sacrificed to appease the deities.[^18chapter15_8] Regardless
of which story is true, this finding “shattered the foundations of the
Pythagorean world, and someone died.”

Is this what happens when one questions the status quo of maths? Is this
doomed violence against the rules what motivates the girl's silly
attitude, covering herself with a mask of naivety and stupidity? Girl
Math makes her point using rather subtle, subversive strategies, because
she intuitively remembers how dangerous it is to oppose big boi logic
inside his doctrine. She is ingrained with the survival instinct of her
ancestors. She remembers how those who strayed from the path of reason
were branded heretics, madmen, witches, and were drowned at sea. She
intuits the cost of appearing irrational in a world that treats logic as
law. So, instead of moving directly in opposition to logic, she makes
logic go sideways. She jokes. She exaggerates. She memes. She says,
"Girl Math kinda makes sense to me, idk why are you so upset, I'm
literally just a dumb and harmless girl.” Arguably, that’s the smartest
thing the girl does: act naive and dumb, because she knows that if she
makes you laugh at her, then you won’t burn her at the stake.

After centuries of mathematicians redefining what counts as a number, a
proof, and even *truth*, we can step back and ask: Is this what the girl
is currently doing? Is she breaking the foundations of mathematics? The
answer is yes and no. Yes, according to the boys clutching their axioms.
No, according to every great mathematician who rewrote old rules to make
new fields of mathematics possible. When a girl explains Girl Math with
examples like "cash is free," "returning something to the store is equal
to making a profit," or "if I book and pay for my holiday now, by the
time it comes around, it's free," she is invoking mathematical power.
Indeed, each of these statements functions as a type of axiom within the
Girl Math system: starting assumptions we accept without proof. She is
also playing with the terms—'profit' now includes returns, 'free' now
includes prepaid holidays—therefore giving new definitions to terms
within her system. From these axioms and definitions, logical
consequences follow: if cash is free, spending doesn't reduce net
wealth; if returns count as profit, every return is a positive
contribution and can be spent on another latte. Mathematically speaking,
she is constructing a conceptual world, a playful version of pure math.
She chooses the rules, explores the outcomes, and creates an internally
consistent system, even if she might later change her mind. Girl Math,
in this sense, is precisely what pure mathematicians do: build worlds,
test their consequences, discover truths relative to the rules they've
set, destroy, build again.

### Who’s the Boy, and Is He a True Mathematician?

Just as the girl is not a human, not a biological entity, not a gender,
a boy is not either. A boy is an archetype, a distilled pattern of
thought and behaviour, a mathematical logic embodied in a persona. He is

The boy uses math with his own agenda and on his own premises, forcing

The Cambridge Dictionary of Philosophy states that "an irrational belief
is one that obviously conflicts with what one should know.[^18chapter15_9] This
characterisation of an irrational belief is identical with the
psychiatric characterisation of a delusion." The irrational is not only
wrong—she's literally crazy. Yet the concept of rationality itself is
surprisingly subjective and hard to pin down. The Cambridge Dictionary
of Philosophy initially defines rationality as a purely normative
concept: "if it is rational, we ought to choose it." By definition,
then, a rationalist is always right. However, the text immediately
concedes that this definition is paradoxical: "often, competing actions,
beliefs, or desires count as rational." In other words, rationality is a
universal rule that's different for everyone.

To work around these contradictions, the Dictionary suggests that within
the rationalist doctrine, irrationality provides a more stable basis for
a universal definition of rationality. All accounts can agree that "to
say \[something\] is irrational is to claim that it should always be
avoided." Rationality, it follows, is anything that is not irrational.
It's genuinely poetic that rationality becomes most unambiguous only
when defined by its disowned twin, irrationality.

In light of this definition, we could revisit the story of Hippasus and
the Pythagoreans and ask ourselves whether drowning the guy was a
rational decision (assuming it was the Pythagoreans who did it). For the
Pythagoreans, irrational numbers obviously conflicted with what they
knew. An irrational number was considered a textbook irrational because
it contradicted their "all is number" belief. Other rationalists might
argue that losing a life over a mathematical argument is irrational, as
it is precisely the type of thing we generally ought to avoid. What is
rational remains a normative judgement: it depends. To fully understand
the origins of the rationality doctrine, we should begin by debunking
its foundational myth: the idea that the earliest philosophical thought
originated in Man, specifically in three Greek boys: Socrates, Plato,
and Aristotle.

## Three Greek Boys

### Boy Math Descendants

[\
](https://networkcultures.org/longform/wp-content/uploads/sites/31/2025/11/16.png)In
Western rational history, the lexicon generally starts with Plato. We
consider him the Boy Math patient zero, although one might question
whether he would have agreed with said doctrine. In Plato’s Theory of
Forms, the philosopher claims that proper form does not exist in the
messy material world but rather in the realm of abstraction. The world
we see is imperfect and contingent, a perceivable reality that Plato saw
as a degraded reflection of a purer, more abstract essence. For example,
there are circular things everywhere: tree trunks, the sun, our veins.
However, the 'perfect' circle is one we can only create in our minds.
This is the core idea of Plato's cave: a philosopher's task is to turn
away from the imperfect shadows and seek the proper Form that casts
them. In many ways, boy math starts here: Plato made a deliberate move
away from organic textures and toward theoretical perfection. The
philosopher believed this type of thinking to be of a higher form than
what was possible in the material world, therefore becoming the daddy of
mind over matter. Trust the idea, not the body. Plato walked so Silicon
Valley could run the simulation.

### Aristotle's Teachings

*“We think we have scientific knowledge of a thing only when we think
that we know the cause on which the fact depends.” - Aristotle in
Posterior Analytics*

Aristotle was Plato's successor, and a worthy, true patient 1 of boy
math, as his philosophy paved the way for Andrew Tate-type reasoning. In
the philosophical history folklore, Aristotle applied Plato’s vision of
the mind over matter to later turn to his own visions of rationality and
ways of describing natural order. In the article presenting Aristotle’s
legacy on the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Marc Cohen describes
how, ‘in Aristotle’s logic, the basic ingredients of reasoning are given
in terms of inclusion and exclusion relations, of the sort graphically
captured many years later by the device of Venn diagrams.’[^18chapter15_10]
Aristotle’s philosophy differed from Plato's in that sense that he
stepped out of the theory of abstract forms and claimed order and
hierarchy in the natural world with the following motto: “Better known
by nature.”

Aristotle had argued that reasoning is separate from the truth,
revealing that one can easily prove nonsense on the basis of false
premises. This can be illustrated by a very simple example: “If the
earth is a donut, then the earth must have a hole” is deductively true
given that the first premise is required. According to Aristotle’s own
words in Posterior Analytics: ‘simply saying that something is the case
if it is the case, by which device, it is easy to prove
anything’.[^18chapter15_11] Aristotle posed that true premises would always lead to
true conclusions. But how would we know which premises are true? The
philosopher insisted that the necessity of finding true premises must
lie in “the nature of things.” But seeking truth in the natural world
required categorization and hierarchical dichotomies. In his worldview,
those natural premises included: the notion that there are fundamental
differences between the sexes and that men were of a higher hierarchy,
as well as a natural and undeniable fundamental difference between
master and slave. Out of the many ways we could have chosen to dictate
categories, he designed the following:[^18chapter15_12]

> “The male, unless constituted in some respect contrary to nature, is
> by nature more expert at leading than the female, and the elder and
> complete than the younger and incomplete.” 
>
> “The relation of male to female is by nature a relation of superior to
> inferior and ruler to ruled.” 
>
> “The slave is wholly lacking the deliberative element; the female has
> it, but it lacks authority; the child has it, but it is incomplete.” 

As the citations show, the philosopher believed that women, like slaves,
lacked the capacity to fully understand logic and partake in philosophy.
In the blog post 10 controversial things Aristotle said about
women, Shawn Buckles reveals the misogynistic premises on which this
theory was built:[^18chapter15_13]

> "When you consider Aristotle’s take on psychology, he saw women as
> less capable of rational thought compared to men. He felt that women
> were more excitable and governed by their emotions, which undermined
> their ability to reason as effectively as men.”

In that matter, we can conclude that Aristotle was definitely a
boy:[^18chapter15_14] his views on natural order read as the earliest red pill white
male supremacy content. Thank the gods there is Girl Math. All this to
say that, when the girls online called their silly economic
philosophy *Girl Math*, I think they had a point.

A boy sees astrology as nothing but extremely inaccurate statistical

crypto boys, there’s nothing worse than being compared to something as
‘stupid as astrology’, a practice that is often dismissed as
‘unscientific’. Indeed, the idea that celestial patterns correspond to
human behaviour isn’t empirically verifiable. However, these critics
conveniently forget that all logical systems are based on unverifiable
assumptions. Within its own framework, astrology is a closed logical
system: it operates through clearly defined entities (planets, signs,
houses, aspects) and consistent transformation rules (transits,
retrogrades). The reasoning inside this system is valid: the logic
holds, even if the system’s axioms aren’t ‘true’ in the physicalist
sense. Mathematically speaking, that’s allowed. Formal systems don’t
need to be *true* to be *coherent*. Geometry still works on a flat
plane, even though we know space is curved. Classical logic still works,
even if the universe is quantum. What matters in math is internal
consistency: that the operations preserve structure relative to their
own assumptions. Referring to the famous quote often attributed to
George Box, in applied mathematics, it is “all models are wrong, but
some are useful”. If we know that we’re all wrong all the time, then why
discriminate between economics and astrology?

When an astrologist says, “Venus in Aries means desire moves fast and
impulsively,” it’s a valid deduction from a symbolic grammar where Venus
= attraction, Aries = action, and their combination = passionate
pursuit. Whether or not Venus actually influences anyone is irrelevant
to the logic of the statement. In short, astrology *maths*: it's a
rigorous interpretive calculus of symbolic correspondences. If astrology
is a closed logical system, a set of rules, and inference moves, then
nothing in principle stops us from formalising it the way mathematicians
formalise any other theory. Pick your primitives (planets, signs,
houses), write down the axioms (what each symbol means), make your
transformation rules (combinations of symbolic meanings), and then
derive consequences for the next Mercury retrograde. It would be an
amusing exercise to mathematically prove astrology: to write down the
whole doctrine in a set of axioms, some inference rules, some
definitions, and a bunch of mathematical formulas, and publish it with
the claim that Astrology is mathematically rigorous. Suddenly,
scientists would pull their hair, bite their nails, see their paradigm
collapse, screaming, “BUT YOUR ASSUMPTIONS ARE WRONG!!!” And then the
girlies would giggle, “All models are wrong, but some are pretty and
useful.”

To take Girl Math seriously is to consider that just because it's math
doesn't mean it has to be true. By speaking in the dialect of formalism,
we expose the political studs that prop up what counts as "rational."
Essentially, a formal Girl Math argument uses proof by subversion,
deliberately provoking the boys to dismantle the logical house they
built themselves. Let's leave the classic "cash is free" Girl Math
examples behind and go up a few levels of abstraction. In essence, what
point are the girls making? What is their larger philosophy about? In
Girl Math, something is true because it feels true, because we are
resonating with the statement, because we need it to feel good, or
because it's funny. In Girl Math, the truth is vibe-based: an "it
depends"-type of truth, built by noticing patterns between things and
contextualising how we feel about them, rather than an external
authority declaring what's fact. Girl Math is essentially 'omg,
same!'-math. It is at this point that Category Theory becomes
interesting. This relatively new field of abstract mathematics, first
formalised in the 1940s by Eilenberg and Mac Lane, was called
"generalised abstract nonsense," which sounds like a perfect basis for
formal Girl Math.[^18chapter15_15] What was so novel about Category Theory?

### Category Theory as a Secret Ingredient for Girl Math

Imagine traditional mathematics, from now on called trad math, as a big
tree. The roots and trunk are the foundational axioms, and the different
branches of the tree symbolise the branches of mathematics: algebra,
number theory, set theory, Galois theory, etc.. Now, before Category
Theory was invented, mathematicians would be solving a math problem
somewhere on a leaf in this tree, and then realise that they recognised
the problem to be structurally similar to one they had encountered all
the way on the other side of the tree. They then have to formally climb
this tree and tediously figure out how to reach the top, while
occasionally taking wrong turns, getting lost, and climbing back onto
the right path. Once their destination was reached, the mathematicians
would solve the problem of that particular leaf and then take it with
them to climb all the way back to where they started. This results in
lengthy, tedious proofs that can span hundreds of pages or a whole stack
of books. That was until Category Theory. If you already know that your
current problem is similar to the other problem, why don't you just jump
from one leaf to the other? If we know that the tree is meaningfully
connected, why show off all the acrobatics required to get from one
place to the other? Just fly there. Category Theory poses: if you can
show that your problem can be translated into another problem, you are
allowed to jump there, take their solution, and translate it back to
your problem, as though they defined the air surrounding the tree.
Needless to say, the type of mathematicians who spent thirty years of
their life on mathematical acrobatics inside the math tree were quite
pissed that these pseudo-mathematicians suggested they could have simply
flown there. Despite the initial backlash, Category Theory's "abstract
nonsense" has stood the test of time and is now widely used within and
beyond formal mathematics.

Category Theory resembles “*omg, same*”-math, as all we need is some
reasonable equivalence between worlds. Two systems don’t need
to *be* identical; they need to be *the same-ish*. It is fine for things
to behave alike even if they don't look alike at all. Once you start
thinking that way, you realise this style of thinking isn't at all
limited to abstract mathematics. The old mathematicians (*trad mathers*)
stayed stuck climbing the same tree, but Category Theory gave
mathematicians abstract portals. The "objects" don't have to be numbers
or shapes anymore. They could be meanings, moods, memes. If they
consistently relate to each other, they can live in a mathematical
category.

### But what exactly is a category? A category C consists of:

1.  **Objects: **A collection of entities, usually denoted A,B,C,…

2.  **Morphisms (arrows between objects):** A morphism represents a
    relationship or transformation between two objects. For objects A
    and B, a morphism f is f: A→B

3.  **Composition: **If one morphism connects A to B, and another
    morphism connects B to C, then A must connect to C.

4.  **Identity morphisms:** Every object has a “do nothing” morphism
    that maps the object to itself.

In Girl Math terms, a category C consists of :

1.  **Objects:** *things* girls care about

2.  **Morphisms: **the *vibes* between things

3.  **Composition: **If there’s a vibe between A and B, and there’s a
    vibe between B and C, then there’s also a vibe between A and C
    (namely, through B).

4.  **Identity morphism:** an object, say a girl, can ponder “*but what
    if I’m right?*” in which case her situation stays the same.

The fundamental difference between Category Theory and *trad math*, is
that the focus is not so much on the things themselves, but rather how
they interact. It means we do not have to spend as much time on defining
the object, but the object gets defined by its relevant connections.
This is extremely powerful for complex systems, such as (and there she
is again): astrology! Astrology apps like Co-star are often coded in
Haskell, which is a programming language based on… (drum roll)...
Category Theory.[^18chapter15_16] Trying to encode astrology in *trad math *would be
like trying to fit a cloud into a box. *Trad math* relies on fixed
objects and rigid rules: numbers have exact values, operations are
deterministic, and proofs are step-by-step. Astrology isn’t
deterministic in *that* way. If you tried to describe astrology in trad
math, you'd have to assign exact definitions to inherently fuzzy
concepts, such as "love potential = 7.3" or "career vibe = 5", and
define rules for combining them. But these rules will collapse under the
complexity of all possible interactions, or they'd reduce astrology to a
rigid doctrine, which it isn't. We would be losing the relational
richness and interpretable freedom that make astrology so meaningful.

Category Theory solves this issue of mathematical rigidity because it
doesn't care so much about the details of the objects themselves.
Instead, it focuses only on morphisms: how things relate and interact. A
planet's meaning might not be a fixed definition; it's instead a
relationship between objects (planet, house, sign, person) that can be
contextualised. Say two astrologers disagree on what Mars means. One
sees Mars as aggression; the other sees it as desire. In a
category-theoretic model, you don't need to rebuild the entire cosmos
just because Mars got rebranded. Instead, you simply open up the "Mars"
box, tweak its internal mappings, and the overall structure of
correspondences still holds. This adaptability is why CoStar uses a
category theory-based programming language like Haskell. They earn their
money by giving hyper-personalised predictions, which require
continuously changing the astrological model they use for your daily
horoscope. These predictions are based in part on their astrological
model combined with your birth chart, and in part on optimisations of
your personal preferences to lure you back to their app every day.

Once you've gone through all this, it becomes hard *not* to see how
crypto and astrology are secretly cousins. Both claim to
formalise *vibes* into *systems*. Both use symbolic logic to track
invisible forces (be it planetary or economic) that supposedly shape the
future. Both attract followers who speak a specialised dialect ("moon in
Aries," "bull market") and believe that, with the correct chart or
chain, you can predict the future. The observed structural similarity
between the two fields lies at the heart of the 'crypto is astrology for
boys' joke, which can be mathematically formalised and possibly even
proven using category theory.

If we consider Astro and Crypto as categories, the girls online claim
they are categorically equivalent. To support this claim, two categories
could be defined and shown to share a common structure. For instance,
in **Astro**, the *objects* could be planets, signs, houses;
the *morphisms* could be aspects and transits. In **Crypto**,
the *objects* are entities like tokens, wallets, and exchanges;
the *morphisms* transactions, swaps, and correlations. Between the two
categories, certain concepts share a similar vibe, and we could describe
this similarity by defining a *functor*: a mapping between categories.

Let us define a functor F:

Astro → Crypto

Planets ↦ Tokens 

Transits ↦ Price movements 

Retrogrades ↦ Bear markets 

Conjunctions ↦ Mergers

A retrograde in astrology is the moment a planet appears to move
backward, which symbolises a time for re-evaluation. Similarly, a bear
market is a moment of collective doubt in crypto, a loss of momentum. To
show that F: Astro → Crypto are categorically equivalent, we need to
shape both the Astro and Crypto categories in such a way that we can
draw a vibe-arrow between each of the objects and morphisms. The beauty
is that we do not need similarity or exact same-ness; we instead need
a *kind of same-ish* vibe.

In this formulation, the *pattern girlies* don't actually need to become
data scientists or cite existing research to be mathematically rigorous
in their theories. They can simply formalise their intuitions and write
down made-up theories. Describing these formulas in dry, abstract,
mathematical language casts the girl as brilliant and correct. In doing
so, she mirrors the "proof by authority" that *Boy Math* has long
monopolised. Contrary to her evil twin, Girl Math is quite aware of the
ambiguity of it all, but alas, she can live with multiple realities.
Where Boy Math seeks certainty, Girl Math alchemises vibes and
contradictions.

### Girl Math is Pure Vibes

So far, we've laid the foundation for a rhetoric we call Metaphorical
Proof. Such proof doesn't demand that two things be identical; it's
enough to show they behave similarly enough for a pattern to hold. This
'Omg, same!' feeling is formalised as a mathematical isomorphism, which
sounds legit. When a girl claims that 'buying something on sale is
basically saving money', she is performing a Metaphorical Proof through
vibe preservation. The math works not because it's true in a universal
sense, but because it* feels* internally consistent to her lived
reality. Girls online recognise themselves in it, and that resonance
constitutes its proof. The swarm relatability renders the theory true.
(*Statistics, hello?*)

This leads us to one of the most beautiful and radical features of
Category Theory: the *Truth Object.* In *trad math*, truth is a global
constant: either true or false, a binary light switch that applies
everywhere in the system. On the contrary, Category Theory allows us
to *redefine what truth means* in different contexts; each category can
have its own internal logic. In the category of quantum physics, for
example, truth becomes probabilistic, an 'it depends'-type truth: a
superposition of possibilities. Formal Girl Math goes one step further:
its truth object is not binary or probabilistic, but subjectively
resonant. In her world, something is true if it vibrates between
girlies, if it holds aesthetic, emotional, or social coherence. Girl
Math's truth object could be defined as: the degree of vibration between
a statement and a subject. Truth as such is not imposed from above; it’s
felt from within. Every mathematician secretly intuits that truth is a
feeling: a proof isn’t proven until it makes sense to you.

This paradigm shift from binaries to vibes also echoes Donna Haraway’s
refusal of the true/false dichotomy. In her *Cyborg Manifesto*, she
suggests that purity itself is the problem, that concepts such as
natural/artificial, male/female, good/bad were never opposites but
co-dependent fabrications. From a Girl Math perspective, a statement and
its contradiction can coexist as equally valid morphisms in the same
category. "Men oppress women" and "women exploit men" can both be true,
depending on which morphism you're following—which node of power you
inhabit. Girl Math merely makes explicit what math has always been: a
poetics of abstract forms. We reimagine rigour. Instead of an objective,
global truth, we propose a vibrational truth. Instead of proving by
contradiction, we prove by recognitions delivered as ironic jokes. In
the Cyborg Manifesto, Donna Haraway describes irony as follows:[^18chapter15_17]

>  “Irony is about contradictions that do not resolve into larger
> wholes, even dialectically, about the tension of holding incompatible
> things together because both or all are necessary and true. Irony is
> about humour and serious play. It is also a rhetorical strategy and a
> political method, one I would like to see more honoured within
> socialist-feminism.”

Long live the queen. Since we agree with Haraway’s perspective on the
fundamental reality of truth, we craft Girl Math Fundamentals in a way
that will hold contradictions. For instance, if we claim 'boys are
dumb', we implicitly acknowledge possible situations in which boys are
smart. If we declare 'cash is free,' we also concede the financial
reality that sometimes cash is valuable. This embedded contradiction
space preserves the freedom of pattern expression, the poetry and play
that underpin Critical Girl Theory. As we construct the formal Girl Math
universe, we choose to embed a fundamental truth duality: *everything I
just said, but reversed, is always also true. *This way, no matter what
she says, a girl is per definition always right.
