#Creative Class Consciousness: Today’s Art of the Working Class

##Sepp Eckenhaussen

There is a general confusion about class composition under
post-neoliberal capitalism.[^12chapter9_1] Where is the working class? There is
also a more specific confusion about the class position of art workers.
Are art workers, workers? While these questions might seem academic or
even trivial, they have serious consequences for the organisation of
labour and the representation of workers. Who and what are we? Who are
our comrades, and who are our adversaries? How to break the wheel of
disorganisation? How to collectivise and resist?

##The Author as Producer

In his groundbreaking book *History and Class Consciousness* from 1923,
György Lukács for the first time foregrounded ‘class consciousness’ in
Marxist theories of organisation.[^12chapter9_2] Rather than describing the
position of the working class as an enumeration of data
(Klasse-an-sich), he explored whether there is a unified standpoint or a
shared consciousness among members of the working class
(Klasse-für-sich). Following in Lukács’s footsteps, Walter Benjamin
wrote his famous text on *The Author as Producer* in 1934, formulating a
simple, sharp principle for class-conscious cultural work.[^12chapter9_3] In short,
he insists that it is the responsibility of an art worker to understand
their position as a contemporary producer, and, given the social,
historical, and technological circumstances, to carefully determine
their medium and mode of production accordingly. For instance, Benjamin
argued that novelists should abandon the traditional medium of the novel
in favour of the faster, more accessible medium of the newspaper, which
would better serve the goal of ‘political progress’. One could argue
that we face the same question today, except that the positive,
universal goal of political progress is replaced by the more fragmented
and bitter aim of resistance against political regression. What should
authors produce in the cataclysmic mess of techno-feudalism?

However, translating the author as a producer-principle to the
present-day context is not as simple as a cheeky, postmodern inversion.
One of the core successes of neoliberalism has been to replace
working-class consciousness with internalised micro-entrepreneurialism.
In the book *Speculation as Mode of Production*, Marina Vishmidt has
shown that this is especially true for artists who happily jump on the
bandwagon of speculative capital and become some of capital’s most
tragic agents. There is, in other words, very little ground to adopt
Benjamin’s assumption that art workers have a (latent) class
consciousness. Before asking “What does the author as a producer produce
today?”, we must first consider: How can the author today understand
themselves as a producer once again?

##The Art Worker

In response to this question, people working in the arts – including
myself – have taken to calling ourselves ‘art workers’. Historically,
this notion originated in the Constructivist and Productivist
avant-gardes of the 1920s, the very same years in which Lukács
conceptualised class consciousness. Its emergence was closely linked to
social realist aesthetic positions during the Popular Front era, such as
the Congress of Cultural Workers in Lviv, where artists and writers
declared their solidarity with workers in the 1930s. Only much later, in
1969, it was taken up in the West by the New York-based Art Workers
Coalition.

I believe that the most recent rediscovery of ‘art worker’ is related to
the rise of social practice art. But it does not necessarily indicate a
rediscovered class consciousness. For instance, how do we approach one’s
‘identification’ as an art worker in the face of the fact that only 8%
of artists in the UK today have a working-class background?[^12chapter9_4] In the
essay *In a Class of Our Own? Art Workers and the Working Class*, Mia
van den Bos writes: the “problem with the term ‘art worker’ is that it
gestures towards, whilst simultaneously revealing nothing about, class.
\[…\] ‘Art worker’ elides different class positions behind the
precarious reality of freelance art work, without recognising that a
class-based selection process has already occurred before the first
invoice is sent.”[^12chapter9_5] ‘Art worker’ could then be just another trendy
buzzword used to improve the social status of artists, in this case,
only by performing affinity with the working class. As an aspirational
fetish in the heads of self-identifying art workers, the notion ‘working
class’ becomes partially or entirely divorced from material reality.

On the other hand, the words we use shape our understanding of art and
its role in society. For instance, in the book *Art Work*, Katja Praznik
argues that the use of the term ‘art worker’ inherently implies a
socialist understanding of art as a commonly available public good,
which is, by definition, opposed to the capitalist notion of the ‘work
of art’ as an exclusive commodity.[^12chapter9_6] Van den Bos, too, gives ‘art
worker’ the benefit of the doubt, arguing that it can engender a sense
of broader solidarity among precarious workers, and prompt conversations
about how class “dictates who can and cannot afford to make, write about
or curate art and therefore what kind of class interests and
perspectives are furthered in the art world”.[^12chapter9_7] The question, in
short, is not one of ‘correct identification’, but of revamping the
historically productive analytical notion of class for the more complex
reality of artistic production in post-neoliberal capitalism, which
could, in turn, empower the organisation of art production in opposition
to the class-based selection process before the first invoice.

We don’t have to start these conversations from scratch. Many thinkers
have theorised contemporary class composition and offered possible
pathways to a new class consciousness that encompasses the strange and
contradictory experience of life as an art worker. What follows is a
leisurely stroll along some of these theories. Where we will meet
precarians, projectarians, cybertarians, cognitarians, and other members
of emergent (dangerous?) classes along the way.

##Precariat

Since the 1980s, deregulation of labour markets, privatisation,
increasing numbers of freelancers, and other trends in post-Fordist
neoliberalism have structurally increased levels of precarity among
workers in the West. Guy Standing, one of the most prominent thinkers on
class composition today, has described the collective experience of
decreased material and psychological welfare as that of the
‘precariat’.[^12chapter9_8] Quintessential to this precarian experience, as I see
it, is a twofold extension of work: ‘sideways’, the separation of work
and leisure diffused by intermittent employment and continuously being
‘on call’; and ‘frontal’, work that is extended indefinitely in the
absence of sick leave insurance and pension schemes. Without job
security, the work never ends.

If one were to make a list of precarian occupations, ‘artist’ would
certainly feature on it. The Netherlands, where I live and work, is
known for its attractive funding system and relatively good social
provisions. But the average artist in the Netherlands earned a gross
income of just over 18 thousand euros in 2019 (compared to the average
income of 32 thousand among the working population), with 80% of people
in the visual arts working as freelancers.[^12chapter9_9] In 2017, a
government-commissioned independent review concluded that art workers
have poor bargaining positions, are often not insured against
occupational disability, have low pension accruals, and have a high risk
of unemployment.[^12chapter9_10] So, even in a country with relatively high levels
of cultural funding and general social safety, many art workers can
simply not afford to do their jobs. This is, of course, not a regulatory
accident that governments intend to repair. Theoreticians like Isabell
Lorey, Judith Butler, and Wendy Brown have shown that governance through
precarisation is at the core of the neoliberal project.[^12chapter9_11] With its
consistently rising numbers of self-employed workers, art and culture
have been the perfect testing ground for neoliberal labour market
policies. Artists are, in other words, at the forefront of
flexibilisation and precarisation, alongside delivery riders, taxi
drivers, and other (mostly platform-mediated) gig workers. If art
workers today can make any claim of avant-gardism, it must be their
membership of platform capitalism’s flex-work avant-garde.

A further important characteristic of the precariat, as Standing
observes, is the high amount of invisible and mostly unremunerated
labour performed. In the arts, we continuously rewrite our CVs and
portfolios, update our websites, submit funding applications, practice,
study, develop skills, and learn - all in our own time. Then, of course,
there is the bulk of socially coded ‘lifestyle’ work which ‘doesn’t feel
like work’: going to exhibitions, openings and reading groups, having
dinners with collectors, social media engagement, and generally taking
care of our ‘visibility’ and ‘relevance’. Again, art workers are not
alone in this. Marxist feminists like Bojana Kunst, Leigh Claire La
Berge, Katja Praznik, and Silvia Federici have argued that artists’
experiences of invisible, underpaid labour mirror those of domestic
workers and other precarious workers in society.

While it’s hard to challenge the concept of precarity, or its
proliferation, critics have contested Standing’s idea of the precariat
as the new dangerous class because they find the material divisions
among people affected by precarisation too great. On the one hand, the
precariat includes people working bullshit jobs (which David Graeber
defines as jobs that are essentially irrelevant in the eyes of those who
execute them).[^12chapter9_12] However, on the other hand, the precariat would also
include the group described as the professional-managerial class (or PMC
for short). The PMC is a steadily growing group of highly educated urban
desk workers who perform managerial or consulting tasks. While their
work creates little to no use value, members of the PMC would not
typically describe their own work as a bullshit job, and even if they
don’t have a steady contract, they are usually well-paid. It has
therefore been argued that the precariat is socially too loose to ever
become a dangerous class. The material interests of the members of the
precariat, these critics say, simply don’t align. This will be a
recurring theme in all of the class composition theories we will
encounter: the conceptualisation of new coalitions, alliances, and
social groups inevitably has to account for material disparities,
alongside a more cultural diversity, among the members of whatever new
revolutionary subject is proposed.

## Cognitariat

A second theoretical tradition that deserves our attention is operaismo.
Operaismo, or workerism, emerged in Italy in the 1960s and ‘70s as a
coalition of factory workers and, to lesser extent, house workers,
university students, artists, and intellectuals.[^12chapter9_13] They rebelled
against the exploitation of workers in the new factories of Fiat and
other megacorporations, but also against the existing workers’ unions
and political parties. They did not demand better payments or decent
housing, instead, they wanted *everything*: autonomy, communist
liberation, and the total abolition of labour.[^12chapter9_14] This foregrounding
of an end to ‘wage slavery’ swayed many disillusioned workers to join
the movement, but it also posed a difficulty in the application of
classical Marxist doctrines. Because what is class struggle beyond work?
Operaismo, it turned out, required an entirely new conception of class.
Mario Tronti, one of operaismo’s leading thinkers, formulated this
challenge as follows: “The cultural hieroglyphs need deciphering: the
scientific jargon must be translated into our own illustrious class
vernacular.”[^12chapter9_15] This kind of translation is exactly what many thinkers
in and after the operaist generation have attempted, resulting in many
unique, provocative, and often hopeful theories of emergent class
composition.

The theory I want to highlight here is that of the ‘cognitariat’. The
word cognitariat was coined in the late 2000s by Franco ‘Bifo’ Berardi
in *The* *Soul at Work*.[^12chapter9_16] In this book, Bifo stipulated that
creatives and other workers in the ‘knowledge economy’ are the new
production line workers, occupying their dedicated spot at a global,
fully digital conveyor belt of ‘semiocapitalism’. This equation of
knowledge workers with production line workers is, deliberately, not
immediately obvious. The high productivity of cognitarians stems from
their freedom to feel unique, creative, and therefore motivated. They
can pick their own laptops, freely compete for gigs, and work in their
favorite coffee bar at hours of their own choosing. But, Bifo argues,
the actual work these people do (customer journey design, project
management, community building) is just as standardised as the factory
work of their grandparents. In other words, the cognitarian is coerced
into cultivating and performing exactly the right amount of instrumental
creativity and uniqueness (no less, because that would reduce
productivity, and no more, because that would make the worker
ungovernable). This tendency of capital to appropriate the very part of
us that we feel is most uniquely *ours* introduces an entirely new form
of alienation. Cogniatrians are not alienated from their bodies, as the
factory conveyor belt worker described by Marx and Engels was, or from
their consciousness, as theorised by Lukács, but an alienating split
occurs in their very souls. Our creativity remains part of us, but it is
estranged from us, because it is controlled by capital. Of course, new
forms of alienation require new forms of resistance. It therefore
follows that the struggle against capital’s alienation today should
prioritise taking back the productive forces of creativity, and the
cultivation of an unalienated, radical imagination.

This matter is, by the way, not merely academic or hypothetical. Nor is
it outdated. On the contrary, its urgency is a key concern in some of
the most important political projects of the contemporary global Left.
In 2025, Fernando Haddad, the finance minister in Lula’s socialist
government of Brazil, phrased the central political challenge of his
Workers’ Party as follows:

> There is also another emerging force, namely the creative forces of
> capitalism. There is a vast literature of the contingent of people who
> sell their labour power, but their power is much more a creative force
> than a productive force. The Italians often call it the ‘cognitariat’.
> \[...\] It is necessary to recognise that its emancipatory struggle
> today depends on the understanding that the non-propertied classes are
> not homogeneous. They have different perspectives. Even from the
> aesthetic point of view, from the cultural point of view. That is the
> role of politics: to forge a common goal between them, something that
> won’t emerge mechanically from the economy and that will only emerge
> from the political activity of a party.[^12chapter9_17]

Like Standing’s theory of the precariat, Bifo foregrounds the role of
precarity and precarisation as the dominant form of government of
contemporary capitalism. Regulatory power over cognitarians is no longer
enforced by clocking in and out of work, fixed low pay, recurring
evaluations, bureaucratic workload, or mandatory team building outings.
Instead, the cognitarian is subjected to a free market competition of
all against all, the benchmarks of vibes and lifestyle, and the
certainty that there is no safety net. In Bifo’s view, this shift in
mode of governance, alienation, and exploitation marks a decisive
turning point in history. Precarity, he says outright, is an era. This
position gives a whole new dimension to the idea of artists as the
flex-work avant-garde. If this is true, it must be possible to catch
glimpses of another world through the cracks in political reality. Even
if art is not here to save the world, the field remains early adoptor of
the flex work in which independently working people have experienced
high levels of precarity for decades. This experience might have taught
art workers some lessons that are of interest to other workers under
neoliberal regimes, providing a basis for new, post-corporatist models
of organising. Let us, then, zoom in on the specific conditions of art
within the global, digitally mediated production chains of contemporary
capitalism.

##Projectariat

The first thing to notice about work and class in the arts, is a
persistent cognitive dissonance. Many artists somehow think that the
market is not where their practice is, and their practice is not where
the market is, despite the extreme proximity between the two. In his
book *Why Are Artists Poor*, Hans Abbing has called this ambivalence the
‘Janus face’ of the art world.[^12chapter9_18] When art and market meet, the clash
between informal, precarious, low-paid, self-organised practice, and the
big money and splendour seen in art fairs, blue-chip galleries, and
auction houses often leads to cognitive dissonance. For instance, I’m
reminded of an artist who told me that she was invited to a luxurious
dinner with the patrons of an exhibition she took part in. She enjoyed
the champagne and lobster, but afterwards returned to eating crackers
for a week at home, unable to afford anything else. She was left with
the feeling that the whole affair had been a mix-up, that she had been
part of something she should not have been.

On closer inspection, the popular compartmentalisation of different
‘sides’ or even ‘art worlds’ does not hold up. In *Seven Days in the Art
World*, Sarah Thornton described how the circuits of artist studios, art
education, magazines, auctions, galleries, fairs, and prizes may follow
their own protocols and habits but are in the end all interrelated and
interdependent.[^12chapter9_19] Gregory Sholette went several steps further and, in
the book *Dark Matter*, showed how unsuccessful artists (he calls them
‘prefailed’) are the dark matter of the art world.[^12chapter9_20] To identify
successful artists worthy of astronomic investments, market players like
gallerists, collectors, and advisors need to be able to discern the
contrast with a much larger group of failed artists. Unsuccessful
artists are therefore the dark matter, the invisible connective tissue
churned out by art academies in large quantities, without whom the
entire system would implode. Art workers, in short, are a fundamental
part of a system they feel fundamentally disconnected from, and that
structurally keeps them precarious.

Rethinking class composition with these systemic traits of artistic
labour in mind, Kuba Szreder coined the notion of ‘the
projectariat’.[^12chapter9_21] The projectariat includes those artists, curators,
academics, writers, assistants, and technicians who earn their
livelihood moving between short-term contracts or commissions, the point
being, of course, that this is the case for the vast majority of art
professionals. Projectarians experience a contradictory privilege; often
highly mobile, they are carried in the international circuits of
contemporary art along with some of the wealthiest of the so-called
‘1%’. But their work is insecure. Typically, they are also laden with
debt incurred to pay for the educational credentials required to access
this mobile existence. Projectarians make up a ‘poverty jetset’, for
whom the spectre of impoverishment looms as each project ends, making
the project the dominant structuring factor in their life.[^12chapter9_22]

Yet, it would be naïve to understand projectarians as mere victims.
Underpayment of artistic labour is a societal norm, but by and large, it
is reproduced *within* the art world, in a phenomenon that Hans Abbing
called ‘art ethos’.[^12chapter9_23] According to this popular belief, one does not
become an artist for financial gain but to fulfill one’s destiny.
Therefore, poverty is proof of the real deal. Conversely, the demand for
payment of artistic labour is, by definition, a betrayal of art. How,
then, are art workers supposed to survive? One of the entries in
Szreder’s book *ABC of the Projectariat* is the sharply ironic concept
of ‘Artyzol’. It combines the Polish term for artist (artysta) with a
brand name for Communist-era insecticide spray, ‘Muchozol’.[^12chapter9_24]
Artworld events generate clouds of artyzol, and the intoxicating fumes
are typically supposed to sustain art workers in place of actual
remuneration.[^12chapter9_25] The smell of ‘artyzol’ evokes the grandeur of
contemporary art but is also slightly toxic. It embodies the strange
relationship between toxicity and intoxication that surrounds the
international art world. Artists may not actively support the
exploitative nature of the art market (and its hostile takeover of
whatever is left of public art institutions), but they silently accept
its terms of engagement, and probably enjoy being part of an exclusive
social circle. In a review of Szreder’s book, Kim Charnley summarised:
“Contemporary art’s heady extravaganza of free market values depends on
banal exploitation. Actually, the projectariat produces the artyzol that
perpetuates its own exploitation.”[^12chapter9_26]

##Entreprecariat

We must, unfortunately, push the point of material and ideological
complicity further. A growing group of (young) creatives identifies with
the creative industries. The flexible and critical thinking that makes
them so creative, and the fact that they don’t want to sit still, make
them extremely enterprising. They see opportunities, and they want to
take the initiative. They find their way – sometimes easily, sometimes
with great difficulty – on the market. Precarious but free, they live
from gig to gig, hopping between side jobs and cheap studios. Silver
laptops, inspirational posters, impressive internships, self-help books,
chai lattes, productivity apps, a 128-page portfolio, and other outward
displays of cultural entrepreneurship embellish their precarious
existence. They try to shape their own lives with the resources they
have: staying positive, seeing solutions, and developing innovative
business models. They might still try to find their way into public
institutions and funding bodies, but also experiment with crypto,
crowdfunding, and corporate assignments.

Thanks to this focus on freedom, flexibility, and self-reliance, the
difference between precarious work and self-employment gradually blurs
until it eventually becomes imperceptible. Thus, these cultural workers
are initiated into the ‘entreprecariat’ of the creative industry, to use
Silvio Lorusso’s apt term.[^12chapter9_27] This is also the core of what autonomy
means in the neoliberal sense: dogmatically staying positive, thinking
in solutions, business models, and innovations, with the inexhaustible
belief that one will manage to make it alone. The members of creative
industries-compatible entreprecariat mine crypto, raise money with
crowdfunding campaigns, create wall decoration for the ultrarich, make
food art for conferences, draw manga on Twitch, make documentaries about
social design, sell ecologically sustainable merchandise, design virtual
fashion, have a following on Patreon or Substack, sell books at
independent art book fairs, and create NFTs.

There is nothing wrong with trying one’s luck in the creative
industries, or with making a good living (it happens). But we cannot
ignore the fact that individual declarations of autonomy in the creative
industries generally come down to dependence on the market, with the
artist in practice often being happy to work for a platform and a
pittance. In the fusion of autonomy and entrepreneurship, the art ethos
– thought dead in the creative industries – re-enters through the back
door: one has to make sacrifices to be able to do one’s ‘dream job’.

This transformation means that autonomy has become the responsibility of
the individual, financially as well as intrinsically. It is permissible
for entreprecarians to retain an autonomous, non-lucrative practice, as
long as one also participates in society in a ‘productive’ way. A
serious problem here is that an individualised notion of independence or
autonomy plays into the hands of managerial instrumentalisation. A
self-regulating sector, where people take care of each other in perfect
harmony with the market (read: where cultural workers divide their
poverty among themselves), is right up the alley of a neoliberal
manager. The entreprecariat, in other words, is the perfect neoliberal
subject.

##Cybertariat

Working conditions on digital platforms like Patreon and Substack have
already come up a few times. It is no coincidence that the rise of
digital technology and the notion of creative industries coincided, and
it became clear early on that the advent of the internet would
revolutionise ‘creative’ labour. For instance, in 2009, Rosalind Gill
published the book *Technobohemians or the New Cybertariat?* based on
conversations with a new professional group that had emerged in
Amsterdam: web designers and developers. She asked:

> What are we to make of someone who says they love their work and
> cannot imagine doing anything they enjoy more, yet earn so little that
> they can never take a holiday, let alone afford insurance or a
> pension? How are we to think about a person who is passionate about
> the creative work they do up to 80 hours per week yet feels fearful
> that they will not be able to have the children they long for because
> of the time and money pressures they face?[^12chapter9_28]

Fifteen years later, web designers and developers in the West are
usually not too badly off in economic terms, but there is still a large
cohort of exploited cybertarians. Web work has grown into an enormous,
technofeudal outsourcing industry with its workforce based mainly in
India, the Philippines, and other places in the ‘majority world’. Their
overlords (Zuck, Musk, etc.) are the members of the small ‘virtual
class’. This class enemy has been described by Arthur Kroker and Michael
Weinstein in *Data Trash: The Theory of the Virtual Class* as deeply
authoritarian, technocratic, corporate, autistic, and
techno-optimist.[^12chapter9_29] The 1994 book remains one of the sharpest analyses
of the mass psychological forces that shaped the early web (social weak
links, instant information, post-historical depoliticisation, global
markets-opportunism, the desire to abandon wetware, and so on) as part
of a concerted political effort to accelerate capitalism. It also
perfectly shows that recent phenomena like the alt-right, the
manosphere, and the mainstream popularity of Luigi Mangione are the
expected cultural projections of the virtual class and those who share
its nihilist ‘Will to Virtuality’ rather than disruptive or subversive
subcultures.

Art workers are not exactly part of the cybertarian workforce found in
click and troll farms. However, nowhere can the distinct downward social
mobility of art workers be felt as clearly as online. While paid work in
institutions is evaporating, people naturally turn to platforms. They
take online commissions to make websites, illustrations, or 3D renders
for a quick buck on Fiverr, harvest engagement on socials, and,
especially, put their hopes in the new subscription-based publishing
platforms like Substack.[^12chapter9_30] Their life turns into a retrace with the
machines, dominated by a simple, persistent dogma: crack the algorithm,
or be cracked by the algorithm. Some find their calling as an influencer
theorist, but most end up as lumpen slop creators.[^12chapter9_31] The art world
has been transformed less by the extravaganza of ‘stupid’ NFT money than
by the extremely precarious conditions of online gig work.[^12chapter9_32]

##Art of the Working Class, Art of the People 

I can imagine that the ‘riot of the -riat’ presented above has provoked
eye-rolls, or at least some nervous laughter. Who needs precariats,
entreprecariats, projectariats, cybertariats, cognitariats,
affectariats, and so on? Why make class struggle so complex? Are people
afraid to engage with, or worse, incapable of recognising, the good old
*proletariat*? Yet, I think we have strategic reasons to insist on the
complexity of class composition theory. If things were so simple, why
are art workers in general so confused about their class position, and
so badly organised? Is this not the classic issue of the ‘little
person’: producing in an extractive system that they struggle to
understand, let alone control? Of course, we won’t be returning to the
days of Great Leaders on the left, who will explain to us how history
will unfold and what our role in it will be. After the collapse of the
grand narratives, ours can only be a Marxism without guarantees, as
Stuart Hall beautifully called it.[^12chapter9_33] Let us, then, allow ourselves
the freedom of organic theory, so that we might glean from different
texts and experiences, and speculate together. The point is not to find
a correct terminology (language) or to engage in edgy cosplay
(identity), but to formulate an effective class analysis that is true to
our time (praxis). The inconvenient truth we can learn from Benjamin's
100-year-old essay is that the art of the working class can only be
defined in relation to our historical and material circumstances. The
work never ends, not even with Marxism.

I wrote the first version of this chapter shortly after the Dutch
general election in November 2023, which resulted in a landslide victory
of Geert Wilders’s extreme right Freedom Party. It was not the first
surge of extreme right-wing political momentum in the Netherlands. But
it was the first time Wilders’s party became the largest in the Dutch
parliament, and also the first time they managed to form a government
coalition (thanks to the liberal party and two Christian Democrat
factions who decided to ‘listen to the people’). In other words, this
election result marked the end of the thirty years of neoliberal
government in the Netherlands. Like many others, I wondered how to deal
with this new reality. What is the art of the working class in a country
where post-neoliberal populists govern over a crumbling welfare state?
How do we anticipate, respond, and organise?

I attended a meeting organised by Platform BK to digest the election
results together amongst cultural workers in December ‘23. I overheard a
statement by Matthijs De Bruijne, an artist I admire for his continuous
dedication to the art of the working class: “We have been making art for
the elites for three decades. Now, we want to make art for the people
again.” His words have since stuck with me. In my mind, they perfectly
summarise the core question of organising art workers in the world
beyond art today.

De Bruijne’s words resonate with a broader trend on the left, which
doesn’t try to defend nitty gritty class notions, but instead asserts
the rights and political power of *the people* as a unifying principle.
A good example is the new French political party La France insoumise,
which has managed to build a strong political party with socialists,
Trotskyists, social democrats, and others from the formerly fragmented
Left - and without resorting to the xenophobic rhetoric often found
among the populist left. The principle behind the party is a new general
collectivism, which party leader Jean-Luc Mélanchon explains as follows:

> The working class is not, in our analysis, the sole revolutionary
> actor of history. It is one component. In our view, the revolutionary
> actor is the people. It is made up of workers and employees, in short,
> the working class, millions of unemployed people who work from time to
> time, millions of young people in training, millions of retirees. Of
> course, you could slap a label on this and say: they’re all workers!
> They used to be workers, they are retired workers, or they are workers
> in training. But that is metaphysics, not historical materialism.
> People are what they are. And yet, this group, the people, is caught
> up in a relationship of domination and exploitation by the
> oligarchy.[^12chapter9_34]

Whatever our class position, we, art workers, are part of the people,
subject to the exploitation of the people, and contributors to the power
of the people. We share common needs for housing, education, health
care, and culture with everyone else. We are, in that sense, entirely
ordinary and nonspecific. At the same time, we possess an important set
of skills, such as highly developed aesthetic sensibility, media
literacy, and interdisciplinarity. Therefore, we may be attributed a
special responsibility in support of a common political project: to
produce art of the people.

I would love to be able to say exactly what art of the people looks like
today. But however long and hard I try to think about this question, all
I can come up with are suggestions and speculations. A minimal
requirement is some form of radical imagination to fight capitalist
realism. In other words, it should render the end of capitalism
imaginable. (This much is easy to say.) Furthermore, art of the people
could manifest as the return to tried-and-tested methods of
agitation—either agit prop or neo-punk. It may be fully embedded in the
political project of building an Online Popular Front. But art of the
people could just as well materialise as the development of new media
outlets to counter the
insecure-white-male-teen-to-right-wing-radical-pipeline, or in the
organising of social unions. Moreover, I would not rule out the
continued cultural and political potential of localist perspectives, in
which art workers support the aggregation of cooperativims and community
wealth building. Art of the people remains, in short, a beautiful
riddle. Solving it, collectively, and in practice, may be the most
important objective for art workers in the years to come.
